*A note before you begin: the thoughts, observations, and research points in this
piece are entirely my own. I gathered a lot of raw notes, scattered writing, and
data along the way, and used AI to help consolidate and polish it all into a
readable draft.*

For about a month now, my mother has been teaching two children. They are the
kids of the woman who helps clean our house. She isn't charging anything. She
offers it freely, out of the simple conviction that she can help, and so she
should. She was a teacher for her entire working life, formally trained, with all
the relevant coursework behind her. Teaching is not a favour she is doing; it is
who she is.

The children don't go to an expensive private school. They go to the school their
family can afford. Their father drives for a household; their mother works as
domestic help across several homes through the day. For a while the kids went to a
tuition centre, but their mother pulled them out because it wasn't helping. The
school itself, she felt, wasn't helping much either. I want to pause on that: here
is a woman who, along with her husband, could not study past the 8th grade because
of their circumstances, and yet she is fiercely, unmistakably invested in her
children's education. When my mother offered to tutor them, she accepted at once.

Over the past month, my mother has been sharing her experience. The children are
young. The elder is in 3rd grade, the younger a couple of years behind. She has
been going through their school notebooks, and their state is, to put it gently,
dispiriting. The way the children describe a typical day at school is worse. There
is no structure to it. The teachers seem either uninterested or under-qualified,
sometimes both. It has the texture of people doing a job for the sake of having
done it, present for the paycheck at the end of the month, absent from the actual
work of helping a child grow. And this, remember, is the condition of a government
school in India. It genuinely pains me.

## What prompted me to write?

None of this is *news* to me. Perhaps seeing it up close, through my mother's daily
reports, simply made it harder to look away.

But the thing that finally made me write was something else entirely.

Today I sat in on an informal internal session at work about AI. One of the
presenters demoed a "vibe-coded" game, a genuinely nice example of using AI tools
for game development. The game was about a rover exploring Mars and collecting
rocks. Then he mentioned, almost in passing, that the game had been built by his
**ten-year-old daughter**. His role, he said, was no longer to teach her *what* to
do but *how* to think, how to understand the end goal and articulate it clearly.
She's been learning to code for two or three years already. His whole aim in the
exercise was to help her define a problem statement and drive it to the right
output.

That is the moment it hit me.

In one home, two children cannot yet string together two simple sentences or do
basic arithmetic. In another, a child of the same age is defining problem
statements and shipping a game. Same age group, different starting lines in life,
all due to the difference in access to education.

## It's a lottery

We like to believe effort decides outcomes. And effort matters enormously. But the
starting line is not the same for everyone, and in India the gap at that starting
line is staggering. A few numbers, drawn from NITI Aayog's 2026 report *School
Education System in India* and the [ASER](https://www.asercentre.org/) surveys it
builds on:

- **Reading:** As of ASER 2024, only **27% of Grade 3 children could read a Grade 2
  level text.** In government schools that figure was just **23%.** Even by Grade 5,
  fewer than half (**48.8%**) could read a simple Grade 2 passage.

- **Basic learning at Grade 9:** In the government's own PARAKH 2024 assessment, the
  national average score was **37% in mathematics** and **40% in science.** These
  are the children who *stayed*.
- **Teachers:** India has about **1.01 crore teachers across 14 lakh schools**, and
  yet over **1,04,000 schools run on a single teacher** for every grade. Nearly 9 in
  10 of those are in rural areas. At the secondary level some states are stretched
  brutally thin, with pupil-teacher ratios of **47:1 in Jharkhand.**
- **The quiet exit from government schools:** Over the last decade, government
  schools' share of enrolment slipped from **54% to 49%**, while private unaided
  schools climbed from **32% to 39%.** Families are quietly giving up on the
  government system, with even low-income parents choosing to pay for private
  schooling rather than rely on the free option next door.

- **Dropout:** Almost every child now starts (primary dropout is down to **0.3%**),
  but the system leaks as they grow. By the secondary stage the dropout rate jumps
  to **11.5%**, right when adolescence, money, and early work pull hardest.

<figure>
  <img src="/chart-dropout.png" alt="Bar chart of dropout rates rising from primary to secondary school in India, 2024-25" />
  <figcaption><em>Dropout rate by school level, 2024–25. Source: UDISE+, via NITI Aayog, <a href="https://www.niti.gov.in/">School Education System in India</a> (2026).</em></figcaption>
</figure>

- **Spending:** India spends about **4.6% of GDP** on education, still short of the
  **6%** target first recommended back in **1964** and promised again and again
  since.

Enrolment, to be fair, is a genuine success story. We got almost every child *into*
the buildings. What happens inside far too many of those buildings is the unfinished
work.

And it isn't simply that we spend too little. It's that the money we do spend keeps
missing the children who need it most. India's education and skilling budget more
than *doubled* over the last decade, from about ₹96,858 crore to ₹1,95,324 crore.
Yet [education's share of the Union Budget actually *fell*](https://www.raysolute.com/budget-progress-paradox-promise-2026.html),
from 3.5% to 2.5%, and early childhood care, the very foundation NEP 2020 claims to
prioritise, grew the least of all. Worse, a chunk of what is allocated never gets
spent: several school and higher-education schemes run **15 to 40% below** their own
budgets, blocked not by lack of funds but by the system's inability to use them. We
fund the buildings and the flagship schemes; the classroom where the maid's children
sit still waits.

And the cracks don't close as children grow older; they widen. India graduates
millions of students every year, and yet employers keep repeating the same
complaint: the degrees don't translate into skills. A stamp on paper, but not the
learning it is supposed to certify.

## Why this stays with me

The maid's children are not lacking in ability or in a family that cares. What they
lack is a functioning school, structure, qualified and present teachers, a
curriculum that treats their minds as worth developing. The coder's daughter isn't
simply "smarter." She was born into an environment where a parent could sit beside
her and turn curiosity into capability.

Neither child chose their circumstances. That's the part I can't shake. So much of
where a life can go is decided by an accident none of us had any say in, the
lottery of birth. For too many children in India, basic education is not a right
they can count on. It is a privilege that depends on which school, which teacher,
which household they happened to be born into.

## The hope is in the students, not the system

And yet. Even as the system stumbles, the students refuse to. This very month,
India topped the [International Physics Olympiad 2026, with all five of its students
winning gold](https://indianexpress.com/article/education/india-tops-international-physics-olympiad-2026-five-students-win-gold-hbcse-homi-bhabha-centre-tifr-mumbai-10783996/).
Five teenagers, best in the world.

I keep coming back to that contrast. The brilliance is unmistakable, and it is not
rare. It shows up wherever a child is given even a fraction of a real chance. These
students didn't win because the average Indian school lifted them. Often they
succeeded in spite of it, carried by determined parents, a handful of dedicated
teachers, and their own relentless effort. Imagine what a generation could do if
that chance were the floor for everyone, not the prize for the lucky few.

I don't have a tidy solution to end on. What I have is a small, stubborn belief,
reinforced by watching my mother these past weeks: that individual attention,
freely given, changes things. Two children now have someone who reviews their
notebooks and refuses to let them fall through. It is not systemic reform. But it
is not nothing.

The talent is clearly there. The lottery is only in who gets to use it. And maybe
that's where it starts, with refusing to look away.
